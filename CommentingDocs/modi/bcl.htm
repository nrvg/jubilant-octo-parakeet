116Dusts, as defined in this standard, are hazardous because when they are dispersed in air by

117any means they may form potentially explosive atmospheres. Furthermore, layers of dust may

118ignite and act as ignition sources for an explosive atmosphere.

119This part of IEC 60079 gives guidance on the identification and classification of areas where

120such hazards from dust can arise. It sets out the essential criteria against which the ignition

121hazards can be assessed and gives guidance on the design and control parameters which can

122be used in order to reduce such a hazard. General and special criteria are given for the

123process of identification and classification of hazardous areas.

124This standard contains an informative Annex A giving examples for classifying areas.

3454.2 Area classification procedure for explosive dust atmospheres

346 Area classification is based on a number of factors and may require informed i nput from a

347number of sources. These factors include:

348 Whether the dust is combustible or not. Dust combustibility can be confirmed by laboratory

349tests to ISO/IEC 80079-20-2. Check on publication

350 Material characteristics for the dusts that are present. These may be obtained from a

351variety of published sources, a process specialist or by testing. Characteristics that are

352obtained from published sources should be validated for the particular application, since

353there are often significant variations in dust characteristic values from one data source to

354another.

355 Nature of dust releases from particular process sources. Specialist engineering knowledge

356may be required for this information.

357 Operational and maintenance procedures for the plant, including housekeeping.

358 Other equipment and safety information.

359 Close co-operation is necessary from specialists in safety and equipment. Although the

360definitions for dust zones deal only with the cloud risk, layers that can be disturbed to form a

361dust cloud shall also be considered. The procedure for identifying zones is as follows .

362a) The first step is to identify whether the material is combustible and, for the purpose of

363assessment of ignition sources, determine the material characteristics. Parameters such

364as particle size, moisture content, cloud and layer minimum ignition temperature and

365 electrical resistivity shall be considered. The appropriate dust group; Group IIIA for

366combustible flyings, Group IIIB for non-conductive dust, or Group IIIC for conductive dust

367shall be identified.

368NOTE Information on dust characteristics may be found in ISO/IEC 80079 -20-2. (check on publication)

369b) The second step is to identify items of equipment where explosive dust mixtures may be

370contained or sources of dust release can be present, as given in Clause 5. It may be

371necessary to consult process line diagrams and plant layout drawings. This step should

372 include the identification of the possibility of the formation of dust layers as given in

373Clause 7.

374c) The third step is to determine the likelihood that dust will be released from those sources

375and thus, the likelihood of explosive dust atmospheres in various parts of the installation

376as given in 5.3.

4215.3 Identification and grading of sources of release

422Outside the dust containment, many factors can influence the area classification. Where

423 higher than atmospheric pressures are used within the dust containment (e.g. positive

424pressure pneumatic transfer) dust can easily be blown out of leaking equipment. In the case

425of negative pressure within the dust containment, the likelihood of formation of dusty areas

426outside the equipment is very low. Dust particle size, moisture content and, where applicable,

427factors such as transport velocity, dust extraction rate and fall height can influence release

428rate potential. Once the process potential for release is known, each source of release shall

429be identified and its grade or grades of release determined.

430Grades of release are as follows:

431– continuous grade of release:

432release that exists continuously, or may be expected to continue for long periods , or for

433short periods that occur frequently. For example, the inside of a mixing vessel or a storage

434silo that is filled and emptied often;

435– primary grade of release:

436release that can be expected to occur periodically or occasionally during normal operation .

437For example, the close vicinity around an open bag filling or emptying point;

438– secondary grade of release:

440do so only infrequently and for short periods . For example, a dust handling plant where

441deposits of dust are present.

442 Consideration of catastrophic failures is not required in assessing potential sources of

443release. For example some of the items that should not be regarded as sources of release

444during normal and abnormal operation include:

445– pressure vessels, the main structure of the shell including closed nozzles and man -

446holes;

447– pipes, ducting and trunking without joints;

448– valve glands and flanged joints, provided that in the design and construction, adequate

449consideration has been given to the prevention of the release of dust.

4506 Zones

4516.1 General

452Areas classified for explosive dust atmosphere are divided into zones, which are identified

453according to the frequency and duration of the occurrence of explosive dust atmosphere.

454Some examples of zones are given in Annex A. Layers, deposits and heaps of dust shall be

455considered as ‘any other source’ which can form an explosive dust atmosphere.

4566.2 Extent of zones

4576.2.1 General

458 The extent of a zone for explosive dust atmospheres is defined as the distance in any

459direction from the edge of a source of dust release to the point where the hazard associated

460with that zone is considered to no longer exist.

461Explosive dust atmospheres from a dust cloud would normally be deemed not to exist if the

462dust concentration is a suitable safety margin less than the minimum dust concentration

463required for an explosive dust atmosphere to exist. Consideration should be given to the fact

464that fine dust can be carried from a source of release by air movement within a building.
